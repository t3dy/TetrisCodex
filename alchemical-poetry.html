Alchemical Poetry Codex

A source-facing section for turning alchemical poems, emblem verses, recipe-poetry, and anthology scholarship into playable feedback. The goal is a response system where the game can say more than "move left": it can tell the player that Mercury slipped, Salt fixed the body, the vessel leaned right, or the wrong gate received the matter.

What We Know

Middle English

Timmermann Corpus

Anke Timmermann identifies twenty-one anonymous fifteenth-century Middle English recipes for the philosophers' stone. They circulated as a corpus around the "Verses upon the Elixir" and later fed into early modern anthology culture.

English anthology

Theatrum Chemicum Britannicum

Elias Ashmole's 1652 anthology preserves English alchemical verse by Norton, Ripley, Charnock, Bloomfield, Dee, Kelley, and others, with annotations and glossary material that can become in-game source cards.

emblem poetry

Atalanta Fugiens

Michael Maier's 1617/1618 emblem book joins motto, image, epigram, discourse, and fugue. It is the model for multi-modal feedback: text, picture, sound, and mechanic all teaching the same operation.

visual sequence

Ripley Scrolls / Mutus Liber

Scrolls and wordless alchemy help us design emoji-first tutorials, source images, and level maps where procedure is read visually before it is explained.

Timeline

late antique to medieval

Emerald Tablet traditions

Compact Hermetic sayings about above/below, sun/moon, wind/earth, ascent/descent, and subtle/gross separation. Best used for tilt, revelation, sublimation, and fixation feedback.

10th century

Ibn Umayl and symbolic Arabic alchemical poetry

Local EmeraldTablet pages identify Ibn Umayl as a major symbolic alchemical poet. His hidden-tablet and image-reading frame fits reveal mechanics, codex unlocks, and sign interpretation.

15th century

Middle English recipe-poetry

The Timmermann corpus centers on "Verses upon the Elixir" and associated works such as "Boast of Mercury", "Exposition", "Wind and Water", "Short Work", "Trinity", and Ripley Scroll poems.

1470s

Norton and Ripley

Norton's ordered craft voice and Ripley's Twelve Gates give Balance Tetris a procedural spine: every zodiac button can become a gate, and every mistake can become an order-of-operations lesson.

1570s-1590s

Charnock, Bloomfield, and English practitioner verse

Riddle, failure, craft, fire, growth, and natural philosophy. This layer is excellent for lab accidents, Rune Bloom growth, and player-facing "you survived, but learn why" messages.

1617-1618

Maier's Atalanta Fugiens

A multimedia emblem-poem system: motto, image, epigram, discourse, and fugue. The game can echo this by pairing source-flavored text with visual block reactions and sound cues.

Draft bark: "The fugue resolves: three voices, one balance."

1652

Ashmole's Theatrum Chemicum Britannicum

The major English anthology frame. Its poems, annotations, fragments, and glossary supply the model for an in-game Poetry Codex and a verified quote/source pipeline.

1677

Mutus Liber

Wordless sequence as instruction. This supports silent tutorial levels where the player reads image, apparatus, and physical result before receiving text explanation.

Response System

placement

Move barks

"Salt receives the burden and keeps its counsel." Plain meaning: the player placed on a stable fixed block.

tilt

Balance barks

"The right gate opens too quickly." Plain meaning: the platform is tipping right and approaching danger.

profit

Receiver barks

"Good matter, bad gate." Plain meaning: the player tipped a substance off the wrong side.

reveal

Codex barks

"The hidden tablet shows its letters." Plain meaning: a beam, source card, or apparatus revealed a hidden property.
